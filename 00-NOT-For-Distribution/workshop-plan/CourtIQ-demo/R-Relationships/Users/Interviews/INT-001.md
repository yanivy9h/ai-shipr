# INT-001 — Player Interview (Ben-type)

## Participant
- Name: Noam Peretz
- Age: 15
- Profile: 10th grade, plays on his school team and in a private club. Trains 4x per week. Active CourtIQ user — 6 weeks in, premium subscriber (his parents pay). Uses the app in the driveway after school.
- Recruited: Via in-app prompt after 14 days of usage
- Interviewed by: Dana Avraham
- Date: 2026-02-17
- Duration: 28 minutes (Zoom)

---

## Key Quotes

> "I open the app, I pick a drill, I do it. But then I close the app and I don't know — was that good? Did I do it right? Am I any better than last week?"

> "The videos are too long. I'm standing in the driveway with my ball and I'm watching a 10-minute video. I skip to the part that shows the move and then I try it. I don't watch the whole thing."

> "My coach doesn't know I'm using it. I want to show him but there's nothing to show. Like, what do I say — 'I did drills'? I want to show him I improved."

> "I hit 20 crossovers in a row for the first time last week. But the app doesn't know that. It doesn't care. It's the same for me as the first day."

---

## What We Heard

**On video length:**
Noam consistently skips to the drill demonstration, ignoring the intro and coaching explanation. He doesn't feel the full video adds value for him — he wants to see the move and try it. Estimated his actual watch-time per drill: 90 seconds out of an average 9-minute video.

**On progress feedback:**
The biggest theme. He has no sense of whether he's improving. He can feel it physically sometimes ("I think my crossover is faster") but has no external signal to confirm it. The app doesn't surface any change over time. He described this as "doing homework with no grade."

**On showing his coach:**
He wants the app to give him something to bring to his coach — evidence of work done between sessions. Currently nothing in the app fulfils this. He mentioned it unprompted twice.

**On what would keep him coming back:**
"If I could see that I went from 12 reps to 20 reps on the crossover... that would keep me going. I'd want to beat my own number."

---

## Insights

1. **Short-form is validated from the user side.** Noam is already self-selecting for the first 90 seconds. Shortening videos to 2 minutes isn't taking something away — it's acknowledging what he actually does.
2. **Progress visibility is the retention hook.** He's motivated by personal bests, not streaks. "Beat your own number" is the frame — completion count matters more than consecutive days.
3. **Coach as social proof channel.** Wanting to show the coach is a strong signal. If the app produced something shareable, Noam would become a distribution channel without being asked.
