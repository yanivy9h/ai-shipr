# INT-002 — Player Interview (Ben-type, churned)

## Participant
- Name: Tal Shachar
- Age: 13
- Profile: 8th grade, plays on his school team. Used CourtIQ for 3 weeks, then stopped. Parents cancelled the subscription at the end of month 1.
- Recruited: Via cancellation survey — selected "I stopped using it" as churn reason
- Interviewed by: Dana Avraham
- Date: 2026-02-19
- Duration: 22 minutes (phone call — Tal was with his mom who sat nearby)
- Note: Tal was initially quiet. The most revealing answers came after 10 minutes once he warmed up.

---

## Key Quotes

> "It was fine. I used it for a bit. Then I kind of forgot about it."

> "There's too much to choose from. I didn't know which one to do. So I just picked one and it felt random."

> "My friend doesn't have it so it's just me doing drills alone in my room. It feels weird."

> "I didn't know if I was doing it right. The video shows how to do it but no one tells you if you're doing it right."

> *(when asked what would have made him stay)* "If it told me what to do. Like, 'do this today.' I don't want to choose."

---

## What We Heard

**On why he stopped:**
Tal didn't have a single moment of frustration — it was gradual disengagement. The app didn't create a pull to return. No notification, no "you've been away", no clear next drill waiting for him. After a few days of not opening it, the habit dissolved.

**On the drill selection problem:**
He found the choice overwhelming. With 45 drills (and only the Foundation level unlocked), he didn't have a framework for what to work on first. He picked randomly, which made the practice feel disconnected from any goal. He described it as "just doing stuff."

**On social isolation:**
He mentioned his friend twice unprompted. Practicing alone with a video felt strange — he's used to training with teammates. The lack of any social layer (even just knowing others are doing the same drills) made it feel lonely.

**On form feedback:**
He was uncertain whether he was executing the drills correctly. The video showed the move but didn't tell him if he was doing it right. This created low confidence and reduced motivation to repeat.

---

## What We Learned

1. **Onboarding needs a guided path.** Tal is a "tell me what to do" user. The open library is a feature for advanced users. New users need a default daily practice queue — remove the decision entirely for the first 2 weeks.
2. **No return trigger = no return.** There is nothing in the current product that pulls a lapsed user back. No streak at risk, no reminder of unfinished work, no personalised prompt. This is a retention mechanic gap, not a content gap.
3. **Form validation is a missing layer.** Tal's uncertainty about whether he was doing drills correctly is a real barrier. This isn't something the current product can solve quickly, but it's worth flagging as a long-term feature direction.

## Insight Flag
The "tell me what to do" finding is significant. It suggests that the free-choice drill library model may not work for players below a certain engagement level. A daily recommended drill — even just one — could change the return rate. Worth a future experiment.
