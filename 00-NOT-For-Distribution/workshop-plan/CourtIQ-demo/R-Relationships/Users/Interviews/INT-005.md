# INT-005 — Coach Interview (Coach David-type, sceptical)

## Participant
- Name: Eran Shalom
- Age: 42
- Profile: Youth basketball coach for 11 years. Coaches a club team of 18 players, ages 13–17. Works as a physical education teacher by day. Tech-cautious — uses WhatsApp for team communication, tried one coaching app that "was abandoned after three weeks."
- Recruited: Via a referral from Omer's network; Eran agreed to talk but was clear he was not looking for new tools
- Interviewed by: Dana Avraham
- Date: 2026-02-25
- Duration: 35 minutes (in-person, at a gym — slight background noise)

---

## Key Quotes

> "I send the link in WhatsApp. Three kids watch it. The rest 'forget.' I've accepted this is how it works."

> "I'm not looking for another app to manage. I already have too many things to track."

> "If it saves me time at the start of practice — not after, at the start — then maybe. That's the only pitch I'd listen to."

> "My players don't practice alone. That's the problem. The app won't fix that. Their phones will distract them from the app."

> "I tried [a different coaching platform] and it lasted two months. Then no one used it and I was the only one left logging things in it. That's the pattern."

> *(when shown a mockup of coach drill assignment)* "Okay. If I can assign this in two minutes and see who did it before they arrive — that changes the first 10 minutes of every session."

---

## What We Heard

**On his current workflow:**
Eran's process: send drill video link in WhatsApp group → hope players watch it → arrive at practice → assume no one watched → re-teach from scratch. He has completely accepted this as the baseline. He has no expectation of behaviour change from digital tools.

**On tool fatigue:**
He's been burned by tools before. His primary concern is becoming the only person who maintains a tool that players ignore. The fear isn't the tool itself — it's adoption asymmetry. He'll invest time setting it up; players won't follow through; he's stuck with the overhead.

**On what would change his mind:**
His threshold is very specific: he needs to know whether players completed the assigned drill before they walk into his gym. The value is in the first 10 minutes of practice — knowing who prepared and who didn't, so he can adjust the warm-up on the fly. If CourtIQ can deliver that reliably, he'd use it.

**On player attention and phones:**
He's sceptical that players will use their phones for structured practice. "Phones mean Instagram and YouTube. That's the competition, not other drill apps." This is a real behavioural barrier — not a product problem, but a habit and environment problem the product has to work around.

---

## What We Learned

1. **The pitch that works for Eran is coach-time savings, not player development.** He doesn't need to believe the app helps players improve. He needs to believe it saves him 10 minutes of re-teaching at the start of every session. That's the ROI frame that lands.
2. **Adoption risk is his primary objection.** The concierge onboarding model (INI-003 invite-only beta) directly addresses this. If Dana is manually onboarding him and walking him through the first assignment, the risk of being abandoned with an empty tool is mitigated.
3. **The 10-minute warm-up insight is a positioning lever.** "Know who prepared before they arrive" is a concrete, immediate-value statement. Stronger than "improve your players' skills." Should inform Yael's launch copy for the coach feature.

## Insight Flag
Eran's reaction to the mockup was the most revealing moment. He went from dismissive to interested in one sentence when he understood the specific use case: assign → see completion → adjust warm-up. The value prop for coaches needs to be that narrow and that specific — not "a platform for your team."
