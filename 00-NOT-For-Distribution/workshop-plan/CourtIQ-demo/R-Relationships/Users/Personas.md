# Personas

## Persona 1 — Ben "Basketball" Levy

- Role / profile: 14-year-old, 8th grade. Plays on his school team, trains 3x per week with his coach. Uses CourtIQ on his phone between sessions — mostly in the driveway or at the park.
- Job-to-be-done: Get better at specific skills (crossover dribble, shooting form from mid-range) without needing his coach there. Wants structured practice, not just watching videos randomly.
- Biggest pain: He has no idea if he's actually improving. The drills feel disconnected — he does one, then scrolls to another, but nothing tells him he's getting better at anything. After 3 weeks, it starts to feel pointless.
- Emotional drivers: Wants to make the varsity team next year. Compares himself to teammates. Proud — doesn't want to be the guy who didn't put in the work. Motivated by visible progress and by feeling ahead of the competition.
- What success looks like: He opens the app and sees that his completion rate on the Crossover Basics drill went from 3 reps to 20 over two weeks. He can show his coach the log. He feels like the app is pushing him forward, not just filling time.
- Where they show up: After school (4–7pm), evenings, weekends before games. Short sessions, 10–15 minutes max. Phone only. Often outdoors — needs offline mode or low-data video.
- Current drills in progress (Ball Handling Foundation): Crossover Basics (20 reps target), Between the Legs (15 reps each leg), Figure 8 Dribble (10 cycles). Completed Stationary Dribble and Behind the Back. Has not unlocked Control level yet.

## Persona 2 — Mira Cohen

- Role / profile: 42-year-old working parent. Ben's mom. Pays for the premium subscription (₪29/month). Works full-time, doesn't follow basketball closely, but supports her son's interest in it.
- Job-to-be-done: Know that the money she's spending on the subscription is actually helping Ben improve — not just keeping him occupied on his phone.
- Biggest pain: She has no visibility. Ben says he's using the app. She can't tell if that's true. When month 2 rolls around and the subscription renews, she has no evidence it's working. She cancels because she's not going to keep paying for something she can't see the value of.
- Emotional drivers: Wants to support Ben's passion without wasting money. Worried about screen time. Would feel good knowing she made a smart investment in his development — not just bought him another distraction.
- What success looks like: She gets a weekly email: "Ben completed 8 drills this week and improved his completion rate on the crossover by 40%." She stops wondering if the subscription is worth it. At renewal, she clicks "keep" without hesitation.
- Where they show up: Email only. Never opens the app. Makes the renewal decision. If she doesn't receive value signals passively (without having to open anything), she churns.

## Persona 3 — Coach David Haim

- Role / profile: 38-year-old youth basketball coach. Runs a club team of 12 players, ages 12–16. Has been coaching for 8 years. Technical, detail-oriented, genuinely invested in his players' development.
- Job-to-be-done: Assign specific drills to his players before training sessions and know who actually did them — without using WhatsApp group messages and hoping for the best.
- Biggest pain: He sends drill video links in WhatsApp. Players ignore them or "forget." He arrives at training not knowing who prepared and who didn't. He can't hold players accountable because there's no record. He ends up re-teaching things they should have practised.
- Emotional drivers: Takes pride in developing players who improve measurably. Wants to be seen as a forward-thinking coach who uses technology. Cares about his team's results. Frustrated by the WhatsApp chaos but doesn't have a better solution.
- What success looks like: Before Tuesday's session, he assigns a specific dribbling drill to all 12 players. On Tuesday morning, he sees that 9 of 12 completed it. He knows who to push in the warm-up. His players show up prepared. He mentions CourtIQ to two other coaches at the weekend tournament.
- Where they show up: Desktop or tablet, 2–3x per week. Before sessions for assignment, after sessions to check completion. Not a heavy mobile user during games or on the bench. Would use a web dashboard — doesn't need the mobile app experience.
