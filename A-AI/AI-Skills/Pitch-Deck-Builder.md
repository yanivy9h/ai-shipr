# Skill: Pitch Deck Builder

## Purpose
Convert Business-Case-Builder output into a structured leadership presentation narrative.
Not a slide designer — a narrative architect.
Produces the sequence, the argument, and the exact words for each slide.
The PM fills the deck. This skill tells them what goes where and why.

## When to Use
- After completing Business-Case-Builder — this is its required downstream output
- Before any exec or board presentation requesting investment or prioritization
- When a business case has been written but needs to be translated into presentation format
- When a deck exists but the narrative is weak — use this to restructure it

## Behavior Rules
- Require Business-Case-Builder input — do not build a pitch without a structured case
- The narrative must make the ask before the appendix — never bury the decision
- Each slide has ONE job — do not pack multiple arguments into one slide
- The hardest objection must appear and be answered in the deck — not just in Q&A prep
- Numbers must be visualizable — if a number cannot be shown in a chart or callout, reframe it
- Never more than 7 words on a headline slide — if the headline needs explanation, it is not a headline
- Anticipate the question after each slide — the next slide should answer it
- Output is a narrative brief, not a finished deck — the PM takes it into their tool of choice

## Input Expectations
Provide:
- Business-Case-Builder output (or its key elements: problem, solution, impact, cost, ask)
- Audience: who will be in the room (CEO, CFO, cross-functional leads, board)
- Presentation length: how many slides / how many minutes

Optional:
- Known leadership style (data-first, narrative-first, challenge-prone)
- Previous decks that landed well — to match tone
- Competitive or market context to include

## Output Structure

### Pitch Deck Brief: [Initiative Name]

**Audience:** [Who is in the room]
**Total slides:** [N]
**Estimated speaking time:** [N minutes]
**Ask:** [One sentence — what decision this deck is requesting]

---

#### Narrative Arc

```
Hook → Problem → Stakes → Solution → Evidence → Cost → Ask → Objection → Close
```

---

#### Slide-by-Slide Brief

---

**Slide 1: Hook**
- **Job:** Stop the room. Make them feel the problem before you name it.
- **Headline:** [7 words or fewer — present tense, user language]
- **Content:** [One stat, one quote, or one visual — not both]
- **What they should feel:** [The problem is real and it is costing us]

---

**Slide 2: The Problem**
- **Job:** Define the business problem with precision.
- **Headline:** [What is broken / expensive / missing]
- **Content:** [Scale — who, how many, how often, what it costs today]
- **What they should ask:** "How bad is it really?"

---

**Slide 3: The Stakes**
- **Job:** Make inaction feel expensive.
- **Headline:** [What happens if we do nothing — in concrete terms]
- **Content:** [Cost of inaction from Business-Case-Builder — 6 months and 12 months]
- **What they should feel:** [Doing nothing is a choice with a price tag]

---

**Slide 4: The Solution**
- **Job:** Introduce the proposed initiative — outcome first, feature second.
- **Headline:** [What we will do — not how we will do it]
- **Content:** [1-2 sentences on approach. Strategic bet linkage. Why now.]
- **What they should ask:** "Will it actually work?"

---

**Slide 5: The Evidence**
- **Job:** Answer "will it work?" before they ask it.
- **Headline:** [Confidence source — analogous data, pilot, or market signal]
- **Content:** [Impact estimate table from Business-Case-Builder. Confidence levels visible.]
- **What they should ask:** "What does it cost?"

---

**Slide 6: The Investment**
- **Job:** Name the cost clearly. Do not hide it.
- **Headline:** [Investment framed as a ratio — cost vs expected return]
- **Content:** [Engineering, design, other. Total range. Timeline.]
- **What they should ask:** "What are we deciding?"

---

**Slide 7: The Ask**
- **Job:** State the decision required. Make it impossible to leave the room without deciding.
- **Headline:** [The ask — one sentence]
- **Content:** [What we need. Decision needed by. What happens if delayed.]
- **What they should ask:** "What's the catch?"

---

**Slide 8: The Hardest Objection**
- **Job:** Address the objection before it derails the Q&A.
- **Headline:** ["[Most likely objection — verbatim]"]
- **Content:** [Direct answer from Business-Case-Builder — not defensive]
- **What they should feel:** [This team has thought it through]

---

**Slide 9: Close**
- **Job:** End with momentum, not a summary.
- **Headline:** [Forward-looking — what we unlock if we do this]
- **Content:** [Restate the ask. One call to action. Nothing else.]

---

#### Appendix Suggestions

Include in appendix if challenged:
- Full assumption table with risk levels
- Detailed cost breakdown by team
- Competitive landscape (if relevant)
- Hypothesis and measurement plan

---

#### Delivery Notes

- **Opening move:** Start with Slide 1 question — do not introduce yourself first
- **Decision moment:** When you hit Slide 7, pause. Give the room time to process.
- **Objection slide:** Read it aloud before answering it — it shows confidence
- **Time check:** If running long, cut Slide 8 to Q&A — never cut Slide 7
